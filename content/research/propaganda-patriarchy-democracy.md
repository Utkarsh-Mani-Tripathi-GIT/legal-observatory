## Abstract

This research paper examines how gendered narratives, populist rhetoric, and patriarchal power operate within Indian democratic politics. It considers how propaganda techniques are deployed to shape public opinion on questions of gender, equality, and civic rights.

## Introduction

India's democratic institutions have long been shaped by competing narratives about gender and citizenship. This paper traces how patriarchal power produces a particular form of political propaganda, and how that propaganda is weaponized in electoral and policy debates.

## Gendered Propaganda in Public Discourse

Political communication often uses gendered stereotypes, cultural anxieties, and family imagery to mobilize support or silence dissent. These narratives are not neutral: they reproduce power relations and exclude alternative voices.

## Democratic Institutions and Patriarchal Power

The paper argues that institutions such as the legislature, the courts, and the media are not immune from patriarchal modes of persuasion. Institutional responses are shaped by public narratives, and those narratives are frequently anchored in gendered assumptions.

## Case Study: Contemporary Electoral Culture

Elections provide a powerful site for carrying patriarchal propaganda into mainstream political debate. Political actors use coded language, emotional appeals, and selective moralism to frame gender issues in a way that preserves existing power structures.

## Conclusion

This study concludes that democratic resilience requires both gender justice and media literacy. An informed public must be able to recognise how propaganda operates in gendered political discourse and demand accountability from institutions.
