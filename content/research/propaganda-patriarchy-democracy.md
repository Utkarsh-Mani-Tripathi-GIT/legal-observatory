## Abstract

This study questions how the concept of womanhood has been strategically instrumentalized as a part of contemporary Indian political propaganda, and that the state is actively creating a sense of ontological insecurity to consolidate majoritarian authority. Using Qualitative Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA), this paper analyzes how rape culture, political misogyny, and populist masculinity come together in the creation of a protectionist state narrative. A combination of political rhetoric about Love Jihad, digital ethnography of online Gaali cultures, and the selective politicisation of women's safety shows how victim blaming and moral policing are armed to enforce Brahminical patriarchy.

Evidence suggests that sexist campaign language is not only an insult, but also a way of re-constituting women as honour-bearing subjects who need a masculine guardian — and thus denied agency. The analysis also shows the intersection of media and misogyny in punishing transgressive women with religion and gender control, and the use of patriarchal rhetoric on honour killing to vilify minority groups. Finally, the paper argues that the maintenance of patriarchal power structures is inherent to the existing model of muscular nationalism, which endangers the democratic promise of unconditional citizenship.

**Keywords:** rape culture, victim blaming, political misogyny, moral policing, patriarchal rhetoric, honour killing, populist masculinity, politicisation of women's safety, sexist campaign language, religion and gender control, media and misogyny

## Introduction

India has a complicated status in the international political system because it is a constitutional democracy that ensures gender equality but operates under strongly rooted patriarchal values that often put women's citizenship as subordinate to honouring the community. Although women have managed to rise to top political positions, the wider political culture remains deeply rooted in a culture of misogyny in which the agency of women is usually conditional (Krishnan, 2015).

This study examines one of the most important changes in this dynamic: the instrumentalization of womanhood in modern Indian political propaganda. In particular, it discusses how the emergence of right-wing populism has prompted a process of masculinization of politics in which the nation is no longer a pluralistic space but a feminine body (Bharat Mata) in constant danger requiring a hyper-masculine protector (Dutta & Abbas, 2025). This fundamentally alters the democratic social contract; the political discussion stops portraying women as independent citizens with rights but as honour-bearing subjects whose safety depends upon their compliance with convention (Oza, 2022).

The implications of this rhetoric are practical: violence towards women is only agitated under selective conditions when it serves a social purpose, thus making women's bodies discursive spaces where national honour is negotiated rather than sites of justice (Big Think, 2021).

Existing literature offers a strong foundation for analysing the interplay between caste, religion, and gender in India, with the control of women's sexuality being key to perpetuating social stratification (Chakravarti, 1993). Historians have discussed "muscular nationalism," arguing that populist nationalism is based on "ontological insecurity" — an existential panic about identity in the face of social change (Kinnvall, 2019). This anxiety is used by leaders to portray themselves as masculine defenders of a constructed friend/enemy dichotomy, where the political opponent or minorities are perceived as existential threats to tradition (Dutta & Abbas, 2024).

## Problem Statement

The key issue this study aims to address is the metamorphosis of the Indian state from a constitutional guarantor of rights into a "masculine protector" that lives on the constructed vulnerability of women. Although traditional feminist literature has been critical of patriarchy found in legal systems, this paper posits a narrower and more immediate crisis — the surfacing of a protectionist trap in which the state actively creates "ontological insecurity" in order to legitimize the surveillance and regulation of female sexuality.

The issue is not that the state does not defend women, but that the political utility of a fearful, victimized feminine population is higher than the political will of empowering an independent one. This interaction generates a nationalism that is "schizophrenic": women are revered as symbols of national honour, and punished as disruptions to national social cohesion when they exercise agency.

### The "Love Jihad" Narrative as Political Tool

The political discourse of "Love Jihad" — a so-called plot of Muslim men to entice and convert Hindu women — represents a serious corruption of the secular democratic state. This study argues this narrative is not a conspiracy theory by fringe groups, but a mainstream tool to impose "Brahminical patriarchy" by stripping women of the right to choose partners (Chakravarti, 1993; Tyagi, 2020).

The state capitalizes on a manufactured "crisis of masculinity" to mobilize the majority electorate (Kinnvall, 2019). By constructing the existential danger of an "emasculated" Hindu male and a gullible, vulnerable woman, political propaganda generates the need to establish the "strongman" leader as protector (Dutta & Abbas, 2025). This rhetoric eliminates the woman as a legal person with consent, making her a biological repository of communal honour whose safety requires forcibly suspending her civil freedoms (Menon, 2009).

### The "Rape Script" and the Ideal Victim

The state is guided by a strict so-called "rape script" when deciding the validity of sexual violence claims. The legal and political machine creates a dichotomy between an "ideal victim" — chaste, submissive, and assaulted by a stranger — and a "transgressive" woman who works, drinks, or moves freely (Oza, 2022; Baxi, 2014).

This study examines how political discourse weaponizes "victim blaming" to undermine survivors who do not conform to traditional gender norms, where their attack is perceived as a result of their "westernization" or deficit of moral virtue (Jahan, 2021; Tyagi, 2020). The concept of the *zinda laash* (living corpse) is deployed politically to insinuate that a woman's worth is destroyed through sexual violation, placing the "shame" on the victim rather than the perpetrator (Lodhia, 2015).

### Digital Moral Policing

This study identifies a significant gap in how "protectionist" violence is outsourced to the digital world. Social media platforms have become infested with "Gaali culture" (abuse culture) — not only due to broader societal sexism but as a "profitable provocation" that political machinery employs to repress dissent (Gurumurthy & Dasarathy, 2022). This violence is a "techno-political" issue in which algorithmic architectures incentivize misogynistic online violence against vocal women journalists and politicians to displace them from public discourse.

## Theoretical Framework

To question how contemporary Indian political propaganda maintains patriarchal dominance, this study employs a three-part theoretical model: **Ontological Insecurity**, the **Semiotics of the Rape Script**, and the **Masculinist Pact**. This framework assumes that the Indian state is not simply failing at protecting women — it is actively mobilizing female vulnerability to resolve a "crisis of masculinity" in the body politic.

### Ontological Insecurity and the Masculine Protector

The notion of "Ontological Insecurity" — an existential worry about personal identity and belonging evoked by globalization and social change — is the primary prism of this study (Kinnvall, 2019; Giddens, 1991). The Indian situation is marked by the erosion of conservative caste and gender hierarchies, resulting in a "crisis of masculinity" within the dominant majority (Basu, 2016).

Populist propaganda does not attempt to resolve this insecurity but provides a "security of becoming" — an imaginary restoration to a glorious patriarchal past (Kinnvall, 2019). This model redefines the populist leader not merely as a politician but as a "Masculine Protector," based on the logic of masculinist protection: the state requires women to be "dependent and obedient" in order to keep them safe, imagining an external threat that must be continuously created (Dutta & Abbas, 2025; Young, 2003).

### Brahminical Patriarchy and the Rape Script

The paper uses the notion of "Brahminical Patriarchy" (Chakravarti, 1993) cross-sectioned with the "Rape Script" (Oza, 2023). Chakravarti confirms that women are the "gateways" to caste purity in the caste hierarchy; their sexuality must be strictly protected to eliminate any "pollution" of the caste lineage. Current propaganda secularizes this ancient panic into a political "script" — a narrative template deployed to decide who constitutes a legitimate victim.

Oza (2023) argues the rape script forces women to prove "abject victimhood" before their claims are considered. When a woman exercises *marzi* (will/consent), especially in inter-caste or inter-religious relationships, she breaks the script and becomes a "corrupt" woman rather than a victim. The ideal victim is the *zinda laash* (living corpse) — a woman devastated by the loss of "honour" to the point of social death (Lodhia, 2015; Singh, 2020).

### The Masculinist Pact and the Female Fear Factory

This study combines Segato's concept of the "Masculinist Pact" with Gqola's "Female Fear Factory." Segato argues that violence against women is directed not at the woman herself but to fellow men — a horizontal display of manhood to signal belonging to a "masculinist brotherhood" (Segato, 2023). Online, this manifests as "Gaali culture," where political IT cells engage in performative misogyny to signal allegiance to the leader and ideology (Udupa, 2018; Gurumurthy & Dasarathy, 2022).

Online trolling operates as a digital spectacle — a constructed system that results in women self-policing and exiting the public arena (IT for Change, 2023). The propaganda machine creates a "Female Fear Factory" by actively participating in "techno-political" disciplining of women who speak up, making public space a male domain (Gqola, 2015).

## Literature Review

A synthetic reading of current literature indicates that the Indian state has moved beyond passive oversight of rights to become an active controller of female morality, taking the place of the traditional patriarch. The review argues that caste-based violence, online trolling campaigns, and protectionist laws are not isolated phenomena but three related technologies of a single political agenda: the maintenance of patriarchal sovereignty in the era of democracy.

This argument is anchored in Chakravarti's (1993) theory of "Brahminical patriarchy," wherein upper-caste women act as "gateways" to caste purity, requiring strict control of female sexuality to avoid prohibited inter-caste mixing. Modern scholarship argues this ancient anxiety has been secularized and nationalized by contemporary political propaganda. The "Love Jihad" narrative is, in effect, an update of Chakravarti's "gateway" theory: the woman is no longer merely a bearer of family honour but the biological territory of the Hindu nation (Tyagi & Sen, 2020; Strohl, 2019).

This anxiety is enacted through "muscular nationalism" and the "masculinization of politics." Scholars such as Kinnvall and Banerjee argue that Hindutva ideology is premised on a "crisis of masculinity" — an existential panic about the perceived effeminacy of the Hindu male in the face of a "hyper-sexualized" invader (Kinnvall, 2019; Banerjee, 2012). This "ontological insecurity" is managed through propaganda that places the leader in the role of a "masculine protector," forming a protection racket wherein women trade autonomy for safety (Kinnvall, 2019; Dutta & Abbas, 2025).

The selective nature of this protection is evidenced by the "hierarchy of victimhood" in political discourse. Dalit women's experiences of violence are frequently overlooked or repressed by state machinery due to the traditional construction of their bodies as "available" and outside the scope of Brahminical respectability (Mangat & Gill, 2024; Datta & Satija, 2020). Conversely, violence against upper-caste women by minority men receives hyper-visibility that serves majoritarian political interests.

## Methodology

This research uses Qualitative Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) to examine how contemporary Indian political propaganda perpetuates patriarchal power by producing "ontological insecurity." The Discourse-Historical Approach (DHA) developed by Wodak is employed, treating political rhetoric not as fixed text but as a social practice that constructs reality (Dutta & Abbas, 2025). The paper extends the DHA framework into a "Multi-Modal Discursive Autopsy" examining the lifecycle of patriarchal propaganda across three levels:

- **Macro-level:** State oratory (speeches and laws)
- **Meso-level:** Media framing
- **Micro-level:** Digital enforcement (Gaali culture)

### Data Source I: The Rhetoric of the "Strongman" (2014–2024)

A purposive sample of 50 influential political speeches and official statements from senior BJP members, including Prime Minister Narendra Modi, within the period 2014–2024. Texts were selected on the basis of keywords including *suraksha* (security), *beti* (daughter), *samman* (honour), and *atankvaad* (terrorism), following the protocol of Dutta and Abbas (2025). The legal text of the "Prohibition of Unlawful Conversion of Religion Ordinance" is included as a discursive artefact rather than a legal document, analysed for how it legally infantilizes adult women by codifying the "Love Jihad" narrative (Shanker-Grandpierre, 2023).

### Data Source II: Digital Ethnography of the "Gaali Culture"

Drawing on methodology from the Troll Patrol research framework (Amnesty International; Gurumurthy & Dasarathy, 2022), this source examines a dataset of "problematic" and "abusive" tweets directed at prominent Indian women politicians and journalists. An "intersectional annotation" approach encodes abuse by caste and religious identity, tracking the use of the *zinda laash* (living corpse) and sexualised abuse metaphors in the online sphere (Lodhia, 2015).

### Data Source III: Media Framing and Emotional Intensity

The third layer analyses media coverage of high-profile sexual violence cases (Hathras, Kathua) through Framing Theory, comparing "justice frames" (rights-based) against "moral frames" (honour-based). This examines how media discourses correlate with political propaganda to differentiate between worthy and unworthy victims (Awais & Al Gharaibeh, n.d.; Jahan, 2021).

### Analytical Strategy

Information across the three sources is triangulated through "Intertextual Analysis," tracing the migration of a high-level political signal (e.g., a speech about "pink revolution") down to form a "digital flashpoint" of violence against women. The analysis identifies "discursive knots" — points at which the languages of national security, caste purity, and female modesty converge. The study follows a politics of refusal, dwelling on the *mechanisms* of power rather than reproducing graphic details of sexual violence.

## Findings: The Political Unity of the "Threatened" Woman

The findings reveal that contemporary Indian political propaganda operates what may be called "Schizophrenic Protectionism" — simultaneously creating "ontological insecurity" about women's safety to legitimize authoritarian action, while actively deploying "techno-political" violence against women who exercise constitutional autonomy. The data confirm that the "woman" in Indian propaganda is not a rights-based citizen but a "boundary marker" of caste and religious community (Chakravarti, 1993; Menon, 2009).

### The "Love Jihad" Algorithm: Legitimizing the "Gullible" Victim

Analysis of political rhetoric reveals the strategic use of "demographic anxiety" to deprive women of sexual sovereignty. The "Love Jihad" narrative operates as a contemporary iteration of Brahminical patriarchy's "gateway" logic, wherein women serve as repositories of communal honour. Critically, this rhetoric is enacted through the legal infantilization of adult women: by promoting "anti-conversion" laws, the state effectively codifies adult women as incapable of genuine consent, undermining their constitutional rights under Article 21 (Dey, 2021; Parashar, 2008).

This creates a "protectionist dilemma": in order to become a protectee of the state, a woman must confess her incapacity to consent. When she asserts *marzi* (agency), she ceases to be a victim and becomes a betrayer of the "biological reproduction of the nation" (Oza, 2022; Tyagi, 2020).

### The "Zinda Laash" and the Hierarchy of Victimhood

The "Multi-Modal Discursive Autopsy" of legal and media responses discloses a stark "Hierarchy of Victimhood." The findings confirm a fixed "rape script" wherein only women who are passive, chaste, and attacked by strangers attain the status of "ideal victim" (Oza, 2022; Baxi, 2014). Media analysis reveals cases of sexual violence and honour killings are predominantly framed through "Moral Framing" rather than "Justice Framing," with outlets attending to the victim's "modesty" rather than the criminality of the perpetrator.

A notable finding is the political deployment of the *zinda laash* (living corpse) metaphor. Political speeches and media headlines frequently describe survivors as having "lost everything" or being "ruined," encoding the notion that a woman's citizenship is contingent upon her sexual purity (Lodhia, 2015; Singh, 2020). When women violate familial moral standards — Dalit women, women in inter-caste relationships, or those in the public sphere — they are victim-blamed in ways that attribute their assault to "westernization" or lack of *sanskar* (values) (Jahan, 2021; Mangat & Gill, 2024).

### The "Digital Brahmin": The Manosphere as State Proxy

Digital ethnography reveals that "Gaali culture" (abuse culture) is not a collection of random trolls but an organised, "techno-political" extension of state power (Gurumurthy & Dasarathy, 2022). Data indicate a positive correlation between high-level political rhetoric and the severity of Online Gender-Based Violence (OGBV). Women politicians and journalists — particularly those from minority communities — receive "highly choreographed" attacks that are simultaneously sexualised and community-targeted.

An original finding of this study is the identification of a "Red-Pilled" Indian youth cohort, radicalized through the convergence of Western "Manosphere" influencers and indigenous patriarchal nationalism. This group constructs feminism not merely as a social irritant but as an "anti-national" conspiracy. The findings suggest that political propaganda actively cultivates this "toxic masculinity" to leverage digital mobs as "state proxies," censoring women through outsourced online crowds while maintaining a democratic facade.

### Systematic Complicity and the Illusion of Reform

Despite progressive legislation such as the Domestic Violence Act (PWDVA) and post-Justice Verma Committee Report amendments, the findings reveal pervasive "institutional apathy" and complicity in maintaining patriarchal norms (Justice Verma Committee, 2013). Victims are frequently pressured by service providers, police, and lower courts to "compromise," with domestic violence treated through the prism of "welfare" rather than rights (Nayak & Banerjee, 2025; Kaur, 2004).

## Discussion: The Architecture of the Protectionist Trap

The findings indicate that the contemporary Indian state has constructed a "Protectionist Trap" — a socio-political arrangement in which security for women is conditional upon the relinquishment of their agency. This discussion argues beyond conventional critiques of "patriarchal apathy," contending that the state does not stand in neglect of women but in hyper-surveillance under the guise of benevolence.

### Nationalizing Brahminical Patriarchy and Voice Erasure

The legislative campaign against "Love Jihad" represents a critical shift from personal family regulation to state regulation of female sexuality. Where Chakravarti (1993) theorized "Brahminical patriarchy" as operating through family structures, this role has been nationalized in modern political propaganda. The state has replaced the patriarch with laws that refuse to regard adult women as constitutional citizens with the right of choice, treating them instead as "property" of the nation that can be stolen by the "Other" (Unpacking the Love Jihad..., n.d.; Tyagi, 2020).

This rhetoric undermines the tradition of the "Argumentative Indian" — the heterodox, questioning self that Amartya Sen identified as central to Indian democracy (Sen, 2005). By prioritizing the "gullible victim" over the "argumentative woman," the state legitimizes a regressive model of citizenship in which women's safety requires their silence (Dey, 2021).

### Zinda Laash and the Semiotics of Inaudibility

The analysis of sexual violence cases reveals a troubling dependency on the "Rape Script" as a weapon of political legitimacy. The state practices "performative justice" — responding with muscular indignation only when violence fits the story of the "ideal victim" (Oza, 2022). This selectivity produces what Oza calls "inaudibility": a woman's claim to justice becomes inaudible when she does not conform to moral standards.

Media coverage exacerbates this by framing victims through the lens of "westernization" or dress rather than the criminality of the perpetrator, policing women's movement under the guise of news reporting (Awais & Al Gharaibeh, n.d.; Rapes can only be stopped..., n.d.). This institutional ineffectiveness is facilitated by police and political institutions that perceive women exclusively through a welfare — rather than rights — paradigm (Justice Verma Committee, 2013).

### Digital Khap Panchayat and Gamified Misogyny

An original finding of this study is the identification of the "Manosphere" as a decentralized form of state power. Social media "Gaali culture" represents the gamification of misogyny, wherein algorithms incentivize aggression to suppress dissent (Gurumurthy & Dasarathy, 2022; Nguyen, 2021). The radicalization of a "Red-Pilled generation" — shaped by a combination of Western influencers such as Andrew Tate and native patriarchal nationalism — produces a cohort that constructs women's public presence as inherently transgressive.

This "techno-political" violence functions as a "Digital Khap Panchayat," enforcing archaic gender cartography through contemporary surveillance tools (Raghavan, 2021). Politicians who ignore this violence or publicly demean vocal women normalize digital ostracism as standard practice. The politicization of women's safety has become a Trojan horse, inviting authoritarian surveillance into the most personal domains of life and converting a society of rights into a society of duties (Vadhulas, 2020; Menon, 2009).

## Conclusion: From Bharat Mata to the Autonomous Citizen

This study was initiated by interrogating the paradox of Indian democracy: a constitutional claim of gender equality coexisting with a political culture of misogyny (Krishnan, 2015; Ghosh, 2021). Through the "Multi-Modal Discursive Autopsy" of political discourse, judicial decision-making, and online conduct, this paper confirms the existence of a "Protectionist Trap" in which security is offered to women not as a constitutional right but as a reward for conforming to Brahminical-patriarchal norms (Chakravarti, 1993; Menon, 2009).

The findings indicate that the masculinization of Indian politics rests on a leader-follower dynamic in which the leader is constructed as a "masculine protector" through the endless production of internal and external threats (Dutta & Abbas, 2025; Coe et al., 2023). This dynamic requires women to be constituted not as equal citizens but as gullible subjects requiring salvation from constructed threats — "Love Jihad" or the hyper-sexualized Other (Dey, 2021; Tyagi, 2020).

The rhetoric of *Bharat Mata* (Mother India) simultaneously deifies women figuratively and subordinates them practically, as biological containers of communal honour (Basu, 2016). The incongruity between popular culture — where films like *Pink* popularized "No means No" — and the reality of courtrooms reveals the chasm between democratic aspiration and patriarchal practice (Oza, 2022).

The paper concludes that real safety for women requires replacing "benevolent sexism" with constitutional morality — acknowledging the autonomy of women without conditions (Boyce, 2015; Nandan, 2022). Whether Indian democracy can tolerate an Argumentative Woman who, instead of being victimized, goddessified, or treated as a corpse, merely and radically seeks to become a citizen — that is the essential question for the future of Indian democracy (Sen, 2005; Jayal, 2008).
