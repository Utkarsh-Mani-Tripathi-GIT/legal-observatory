## The Weaponization of Human Rights: When Morality Becomes a Geopolitical Tool

![Image](/images/weaponization/image4.png)

March 2022. The UN General Assembly votes 141 to 5 to condemn a violation of sovereignty. The speeches afterward are extraordinary. Aggression must never be rewarded. Borders must be respected. Nobody in that room sounds confused about right and wrong. Nobody hedges.

A year before that, different borders. A different population pushed out of their homes. Same chamber. This time, a statement of concern, carefully worded, nothing more. No emergency session. No foreign ministers flying capital to capital. Concern, expressed, filed, gone.

I'm not going to sit here and argue about which of those two situations deserved more outrage, because that's a trap and I already know where it leads. What actually interests me is the shape of the pattern. Why does the machinery of international indignation switch on completely for some victims and barely stir for others? Severity? Or is it simpler than that. Is it about who's doing the violating, and to whom.

Once you sit with that question it doesn't really let go of you.

Human rights was supposed to be the one place where power didn't get to write the rules. That was the whole point in 1948. A war had just shown the world exactly what happens when states decide certain humans matter less than others, and out of that came a document meant to draw a line no government could cross without paying for it. Life. Dignity. No torture. No arbitrary detention. Not negotiable, not contextual, not something you get to waive because of an alliance.

![Image](/images/weaponization/image1.png)

It's a beautiful document, and I mean that with zero irony. Read it slowly some time. Not like a lawyer hunting for clauses, like a person imagining what the world would look like if every government actually meant it.

They don't. Almost none of them, consistently. But that's not the interesting part, because violations were always going to happen, institutions are made of people and people are not saints. What's interesting is which violations get named. Which ones get punished, sanctioned into economic paralysis. And which ones just dissolve into the noise of a news cycle that has already decided, somehow, without anyone announcing it, to look somewhere else.

Sudan. Since 2023, a civil war has produced what several humanitarian organizations call one of the largest displacement crises on the planet right now. Famine conditions, documented, in multiple regions. Reports of ethnically targeted violence in Darfur, and if that phrase gives you déjà vu, it should, because it echoes the atrocities of twenty years earlier almost exactly. Ask a well-read, politically engaged person to name three facts about the war in Sudan. Most can't. Not because they're indifferent. Because the war in Sudan never got the oxygen that turns a catastrophe into a cause. No hashtag. No wall-to-wall coverage. No world leader photographed at a border holding a blanket for the cameras.

Now put Ukraine next to it. Immediate coverage. Sustained. Emotionally rich, personalized down to individual families, individual soldiers with names and faces. I need to be careful here, because I am not saying that coverage was undeserved. It wasn't. What I'm asking is why Sudan's wasn't comparable. Is a child fleeing Darfur worth less attention than a child fleeing Mariupol? Nobody would say yes if you asked them directly. The coverage volumes answer the question anyway, quietly, every day, without anyone having to say it out loud.

People want to make this a story about racism, about Western hypocrisy, a clean morality play with villains and victims already assigned. Sometimes there's real truth buried in that story. But it's too easy, and easy stories tend to fall apart the moment they meet the full picture.

The fuller picture has more to do with proximity and strategic stakes than pure prejudice. Ukraine borders NATO members. A Russian victory there rewrites European security in ways that matter immediately to Washington and Brussels. Sudan doesn't touch those calculations, not in the same way, no matter how much human tragedy is unfolding inside it. Coverage follows interest. Not suffering. Writing that sentence is uncomfortable. Sitting with it is worse. I still think it's true.

So call this the first admission worth making honestly: human rights advocacy, at the level of states and institutions, was never purely about human rights. It has always ridden inside a vehicle built for something else, strategic interest, mostly, and the only real question left is whether we're willing to say that plainly or whether the comfort of pretending otherwise is worth more to us.

Sudan isn't even the hardest example. Not close.

The Uyghur population in Xinjiang. Multiple UN reports. Leaked government documents. Independent investigations documenting mass detention, forced labor allegations, and a 2022 report from the UN's own Human Rights Office describing what could amount to crimes against humanity. That's not a fringe claim floating around some corner of the internet. It's the UN's own apparatus saying it.

And still, look at how selectively even this gets used. Governments that sanction over Xinjiang keep deep trade relationships with China running in nearly every other domain, at the same time, without much apparent friction. The loudest Western voices on Uyghur rights go quiet, conveniently, on Palestinians under occupation. On Kashmir. On how their own countries treat asylum seekers at the border. And the loudest voices on Palestine, somehow, rarely have much to say about Xinjiang, or Iran's crackdowns on protesters, or Yemen.

Nobody's hands are clean. Not a cynical statement. Just an accurate one.

Human rights language has become something closer to a currency than a principle. Spent where it buys something. Hoarded where spending it would cost an alliance, a trade deal, a military basing agreement.

Afghanistan gives you a slightly different version of the same problem. Since 2021, the erosion of women's rights under the Taliban, girls banned from secondary school, restrictions on movement and employment, has drawn condemnation from nearly every government and institution capable of condemning things. That condemnation isn't wrong, actually. It might be one of the more legitimate, broadly shared moral positions left in contemporary geopolitics.

But here's the thing nobody likes to sit with. The same governments condemning it were occupying Afghanistan twenty years earlier, and the withdrawal that preceded the Taliban's return happened with a chaos that helped create the exact vacuum the Taliban walked into. Condemnation without accountability is a strange kind of morality. It lets you feel the outrage while somehow never touching the part where you helped build the conditions for it.

Selective memory might be the real engine running underneath all of this. States don't just apply human rights selectively across geography. They apply it selectively across time, too. An enemy state's crimes get remembered forever, catalogued, cited for decades. An ally's crimes get forgotten inside a single news cycle, reframed as regrettable but necessary, or simply never covered at all.

Yemen. A war that has, by most humanitarian estimates, produced one of the worst civilian death tolls of any conflict this century, driven substantially by a coalition that includes close Western security partners. Arms sales kept flowing through the worst of it. Public outrage, by comparison, stayed almost silent. Not because those deaths mattered less in some cosmic sense. Because the perpetrator was a security partner, not a designated adversary, and the moral vocabulary that gets applied instantly to an adversary moves a lot slower, if it moves at all, toward a partner.

Hypocrisy? Probably. The more useful question is what that hypocrisy does to the credibility of the whole apparatus.

![Image](/images/weaponization/image5.png)

International law and human rights doctrine were never really about enforcement mechanisms, if we're honest about it. The International Criminal Court has no army. The UN can't compel a permanent Security Council member to do anything it doesn't want to, because any of the five can simply veto action against themselves or their allies. The entire structure runs on legitimacy. On a shared belief that the rules apply to everyone, so even the powerful feel some cost, reputational if nothing else, for crossing them.

Crack that belief and the structure starts hollowing out from the inside. Not dramatically. Slowly. A quiet loss of faith that makes the next violation easier to justify, because why respect a standard that only ever seems to bind you and never the people condemning you for breaking it.

Which is maybe why Gaza has become such a combustible case. More combustible, arguably, than anything else in the last two decades. Not because the facts are simple, they aren't, and anyone reducing this to a clean binary in either direction either isn't paying attention or isn't being honest. The humanitarian toll following the war that began after the October 2023 attacks has been documented extensively by international bodies, and the legal questions, proportionality, civilian protection under international humanitarian law, the obligations of an occupying power, are genuinely disputed among serious legal scholars. Not manufactured controversies. Real ones. At the same time, the October 2023 attacks involved documented atrocities against civilians that nobody serious tries to explain away. Both things are true at once. A huge share of public discourse fails simply because it demands you pick one and abandon the other.

What makes Gaza a stress test isn't the conflict itself. It's the reaction to the reaction. Governments that spoke instantly, unequivocally, about violations of international law in Ukraine spent, in Gaza's case, a lot of diplomatic energy on caveats and calls for restraint that stopped well short of the language they'd used elsewhere for comparable civilian casualty figures. Meanwhile other governments, other commentators, who scrutinize Israel's conduct intensely have shown far less intensity toward Syria's use of chemical weapons on its own population. Or Russia in Chechnya, decades back. Or China in Xinjiang.

Everyone has a conflict where their moral clarity goes suddenly, conveniently blurry.

I'm not saying this to produce despair, or to suggest giving up on the language of rights entirely, because that conclusion is lazy, and honestly, dangerous. Cynicism is its own trap. Convince yourself human rights talk is nothing but strategic fiction and you've just handed a gift to every government that would prefer no constraints on its behavior at all. That's not a neutral position to land on. It has consequences, and those consequences fall on exactly the people the framework was supposed to protect.

So where does that leave things.

Somewhere harder than either "human rights are sacred and universal" or "human rights are a cynical tool." Both too clean. The truth lives in the friction between them, genuinely valuable and genuinely instrumentalized at the same time, and pretending only one of those is happening at any given moment is how you end up either naive or completely nihilistic.

![Image](/images/weaponization/image3.png)

Take the Responsibility to Protect doctrine, adopted by the UN in 2005, meant to establish that sovereignty isn't a shield behind which a government commits mass atrocities against its own people. In principle it closed a gap that let genocides like Rwanda's unfold while the world claimed it had no legal basis to intervene. In practice, R2P got invoked in Libya in a way many governments now think was stretched well past its mandate, and basically ignored in Syria, ignored with the Rohingya in Myanmar, ignored anywhere intervention would mean confronting a state with real military capacity or a powerful patron behind it.

Myanmar is worth pausing on, because it shows something specific about how visibility gets built, or doesn't. The 2017 campaign against the Rohingya, which the UN's own fact-finding mission described in language approaching genocide, got a wave of coverage. Then it evaporated. Fast. Compare that half-life to the half-life of a story involving a strategically central state. Some atrocities get a single news cycle. Others get years. The severity of what actually happened doesn't reliably predict which is which, and that alone should bother you more than it probably does.

Which brings up something underdiscussed, media framing, and how it decides which victims even become legible to a global audience in the first place. A victim needs a story a newsroom can tell efficiently. Images that translate. A narrative arc the audience already recognizes. Ukraine slotted into an existing Cold War template almost instantly, familiar aggressor, familiar sympathetic victim, familiar stakes. Sudan doesn't fit any template most Western newsrooms keep on hand. Neither does the Democratic Republic of Congo, where a conflict that's killed millions over decades gets, in any given month, a fraction of the coverage one week of a more legible war receives.

![Image](/images/weaponization/image2.png)

Visibility isn't a neutral reflection of severity. It's manufactured. Partly by algorithms rewarding engagement. Partly by editorial decisions about what audiences will find compelling. Partly by government messaging operations working, actively, to keep some stories lit and others in the dark.

Which raises an uncomfortable question about agency, honestly. Are people in powerful democracies actually indifferent to Sudan? Or have they simply never been handed a version of that story capable of reaching them at all. I suspect mostly the latter, and that should trouble you more, not less, because it means outrage itself has become something manufactured and deployed, not something felt.

Iran, a different angle entirely. The 2022 protests after Mahsa Amini died in police custody produced genuine, widespread condemnation and sanctions, more sustained attention than a lot of simultaneous human rights crises got. Why did this one break through? Partly because it fit a narrative Western governments already had strong incentive to amplify, given existing tension with Iran over its nuclear program and its regional alliances. I'm not saying the protests or the condemnation were insincere. I'm saying sincerity and strategic convenience aren't mutually exclusive, and untangling the two is harder than most commentary lets on.

That's really the heart of it. Rarely a question of manufactured outrage versus authentic outrage. Almost always both, tangled so completely that even the people feeling the outrage couldn't honestly tell you where one ends and the other starts.

Then there's the question of alliances, which nobody wants to talk about honestly, myself included, because it's uncomfortable for everyone. Alliances function like a kind of moral insurance policy. Security guarantee from a permanent Security Council member? Major arms supplier? Critical trade partner? Your human rights record gets discussed in hushed, careful language, if it gets discussed at all. No patron, and the same violation gets full public prosecution in the court of world opinion. This isn't a conspiracy, nobody's in a room deciding it. It emerges from thousands of small decisions, each defensible on its own, that add up to a system where the price of accountability tracks the cost of confronting the criminal, not the severity of the crime.

Watch how differently the same word, genocide, gets used depending on who's using it and about whom. Governments will spend years arguing over whether a set of facts legally qualifies when the perpetrator is an ally, demanding a standard of proof that borders on impossible. The same governments reach for the word within days when the perpetrator is an adversary. The word hasn't changed meaning. Its use has become a function of relationships, not facts, and if that doesn't worry you a little, it probably should.

Soft power complicates things further still. States manage not just their own human rights image but their allies' and adversaries', through research funding, through relationships with journalists, through diplomatic weight thrown behind which situations get formally investigated. Enough money and reach, over enough years, and a country can shape which of its actions become the subject of a UN fact-finding mission and which never make the agenda at all. Not paranoia. Just how these institutions work when they're staffed by member states who each arrive with their own homework already done.

Is international law meaningless, then? No, and I want to resist that easy nihilism. The Geneva Conventions still shape battlefield conduct, imperfectly, but they shape it. The ICC, gaps and all, still produces investigations that wouldn't otherwise exist, still leaves a paper trail that occasionally, years later, catches up with people who assumed they were untouchable. Sanctions, inconsistent as they are, still cost something real. None of that is nothing.

But none of it is what it claims to be, either. Not a universal, evenly applied system of accountability. A system with real teeth for the weak and mostly symbolic ones for the strong, adjusted at the margins by how useful a given act of accountability happens to be to whichever powerful state is positioned to enforce it.

Maybe the honest description of international human rights law isn't law at all, in the domestic sense. Maybe it's a shared vocabulary powerful actors use to legitimize decisions they were already inclined to make for other reasons. Intervention serves the strategy, human rights supplies the cover. Restraint serves the strategy, sovereignty and non-interference supply it instead. Same government, both moves, sometimes within the same year, no apparent sense of contradiction.

Here's a question worth asking yourself directly, because I think the answer matters more than anything else in this piece. When you feel outrage about a human rights violation, how much of that did you generate yourself, from evidence you evaluated independently? And how much was handed to you, pre-packaged, by an information environment that had already decided which atrocities deserved your attention this month?

Most people can't answer that honestly without some discomfort. Myself included.

None of this is an argument for detachment. If anything it argues the opposite, for a more demanding, more suspicious kind of moral attention. Not just is this wrong, but why am I being shown this particular wrong, right now, by this particular source, and what does someone gain from my outrage landing here instead of somewhere else.

The tragedy isn't that human rights language gets used strategically. Every powerful idea in history gets used strategically, that's partly what makes it powerful in the first place. The tragedy is what happens to the people who aren't currently useful to anyone's strategy. The Sudanese family with no oil, no nuclear program, no NATO border, no algorithmic hook. The Yemeni child whose death doesn't implicate a security partnership anyone wants examined too closely. Their suffering is exactly as real as the suffering that makes headlines. Exactly as deserving of the vocabulary of rights. The vocabulary just doesn't find its way to them, because vocabulary, like everything else, follows power.

So what do we actually do with that.

I don't have a clean answer. I distrust anyone who hands you one. What I have is smaller, and more honest: pay attention to the pattern, not the instance. Notice which crises get the language of human rights and which get the language of complexity, of context, of regrettable necessity. Notice whose atrocities get investigated and whose get quietly shelved. Notice how fast a story disappears the moment it stops being strategically convenient to keep alive.

Because the moment you can see the pattern, you stop being a passive recipient of manufactured outrage. You become something closer to an actual moral observer. Smaller victory than fixing the system, sure. Maybe the only one available to any of us right now.

Here's the question I keep circling back to, the one without a comfortable answer. If universal rights only ever apply universally in theory and selectively in practice, at what point does the selectivity become the real doctrine, and universality just the story told to make the selectivity feel acceptable.

I don't know. I'm not sure anyone fully does. But I think it's worth sitting with, long after you've stopped reading this.
